Thoughts on Accessibility

Accessibility on the web is an important next step towards a more considerate and efficient future. The primary focus is to ensure that disabled and/or elderly people are able to access the same information as an abled user. In doing so, there are often greater benefits that extend beyond the particular demographic that an accessibility function was intended for.

It's extremely important to ensure that the greatest number of users are able to comfortably use a website to its full functionality. Systems in place that bar or impede certain demographics are a hinderance that is easy to overlook if the people putting these systems in place fail to be properly considerate. When technology is created for a specific demographic, it often excludes those it was not intended for (intentionally or not).

Thoughts on Progressive Enhancement

Progressive enhancement is a concept similar to graceful degredation - both are concerned with how a site functions on a variety of devices, especially as technology progresses. The key difference is in the way that the two concepts approach the question.

Graceful degredation tackles the problem from the top by starting with the most optimal browsing experience (a good desktop or laptop with a modern browser) and builds for that. Testing on older browsers and devices is a secondary consideration, ensuring only minimum functionality. Progressive enhancement starts from the bottom and works up - it is content-focused, prioritizing making as much accessible to as many devices as possible by building additional functionality instead of designing the "best" site and then working backwards.

This article helps to lay out the differences between the two concepts in more plain language, comparing them directly.
